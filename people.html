What They Were
Actually Asking For

1. Divestment

Students asked UCLA to divest its endowment from companies with ties to the Israeli military. This is a standard form of official pressure with a long history in American campus activism the same type of pressure used during anti-apartheid campaigns in the 1980s. It is a financial and political request, not a threat.

2. A Ceasefire Call

Students asked the university to issue a statement calling for a ceasefire in Gaza. This is a form of official speech, not action. Dozens of cities, religious denominations, and academic organizations had issued similar statements. The demand is identical in kind to requests made of universities on other humanitarian crises.

What they did not demand

Violence. Harm to individuals. The expulsion of any student or faculty member. The silencing of opposing views. None of these appeared in the encampment's published demands or documented communications.

The Erasure Inside the Erasure

Jewish students participated in the UCLA encampment in significant numbers. The "antisemitic mob" framing made them disappear twice.

First, they were made invisible by a narrative that defined Jewish identity as incompatible with pro-Palestinian protest. Second, their presence directly contradicted the central charge used to undermine the encampment so they were simply left out of coverage. According to the Georgetown Bridge Initiative's 2024 Islamophobia report, this mischaracterization occurred at the official level, including in congressional hearings, not just in media coverage.

"Does this match what you were told? If not, what does that tell you about the telling?"

This is not a political argument about the conflict in Gaza. People hold genuinely different views on that question, and this project does not ask you to resolve it. This is an argument about something more specific: how language works, and what it makes possible.


The students at the UCLA encampment had names, majors, reasons for being there. They had demands that were clear political speech. They were attacked and received no protection. Then they were arrested and the world largely accepted it. None of that required violence from them. It only required that the right words had already been said about them.


David Livingstone Smith wrote that dehumanization is a pattern, not an exception. The next time you see a group described in the language of monsters (wanton, corrupt, terrifying) ask what evidence that language is standing in for. Ask what it is making possible. Ask who benefits from you not asking.
