The Machine

Dehumanization is not just an insult. It is a process — one with identifiable mechanics. David Livingstone Smith mapped those mechanics in Making Monsters. They are visible in the UCLA sequence.

Smith's Framework — Plain Language

David Livingstone Smith argues in Making Monsters that dehumanization is not simply name-calling or prejudice. It is a specific cognitive and rhetorical operation: the reclassification of human beings into a category that looks human but is treated as fundamentally subhuman — dangerous, degenerate, and requiring containment.

This reclassification does something precise: it removes the target from the moral community. Once someone is outside that community, the normal obligations that govern how we treat people — protection, due process, the right to be heard — no longer apply to them. Violence against them becomes not just permissible but logical.

Smith is not describing edge cases or historical atrocities alone. He is describing a pattern that repeats, using language as its primary tool. The words come first. The consequences follow.

Two Concepts Applied to UCLA

Smith, Making Monsters — p. 194

"Monstrous fusions of humanity and subhumanity: wanton, degenerate, and terrifying."

— David Livingstone Smith, Making Monsters, p. 194

Applied to UCLA

The Terrorist Student

The "terrorist" label applied to UCLA protesters is a textbook example of this fusion. Calling a student a terrorist does not deny that they are human — it claims that their humanity is contaminated. They look like students. They walk through the same campus. But they are wanton (without legitimate grievance), degenerate (outside the bounds of acceptable political action), and terrifying (requiring suppression rather than response).


This fusion is what made Steve Garvey's "terrorist" label so effective and so dangerous. It did not ask audiences to think carefully about what the encampment was doing or asking for. It asked them to feel threatened — and feeling threatened requires no evidence.

Smith, Making Monsters — p. 209

"The idea of a natural hierarchy provided an elegant solution to the problem of interspecies violence."

— David Livingstone Smith, Making Monsters, p. 209

Applied to UCLA

The Hierarchy That Made Arrests Legible

When UCLA students were reclassified as "terrorists" and "mobs," they were placed in a hierarchy below the category of citizens entitled to assembly rights. This reclassification is what Smith means by the "elegant solution" — once someone is below you in the hierarchy, violence against them is not violence in the morally significant sense. It is management. Containment. Order restoration.


This explains the two-night sequence at UCLA. On May 1, when counterprotesters attacked the encampment for two hours, police stood by. The students had been framed as threats — they were outside the category deserving protection. On May 2, riot police moved against those same students. The language had already resolved the ethical question: these were not people being arrested. They were threats being removed.

The Four-Step Process — UCLA, April–May 2024

Step 01

Name the Monster

Politicians and media apply the "terrorist" label to students before any documented violence. The category is established in language.

›
Step 02

Erase the Grievance

The demands of the encampment — divestment, ceasefire — are never seriously engaged. The monster has no legitimate grievance, only threat.

›
Step 03

Reclassify the Target

Students are placed outside the category of citizens deserving protection. When attacked on May 1, no intervention comes. The hierarchy is enforced.

›
Step 04

The Consequences Arrive

200+ arrests. The audience, prepared by weeks of monster-language, accepts this as reasonable. The machine has done its work.

"Once someone has been named a monster, the question of whether to protect them disappears. The question becomes only: how do you stop them?"
